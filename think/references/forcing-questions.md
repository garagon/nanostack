# Six Forcing Questions — Deep Reference

These questions are designed to be uncomfortable. If they're easy to answer, either the idea is genuinely strong or the answers are superficial. Probe deeper.

---

## 1. Demand Reality

**The question:** "Who is actively trying to solve this problem right now, and how do you know?"

**What good answers look like:**
- "I talked to 15 users at [company type] and 12 described this exact pain"
- "I see 500+ threads on [forum] asking for this with no good answer"
- "I experience this pain myself every day at work"

**Red flags:**
- "Everyone would want this" — too broad, probably nobody wants it enough
- "The market is $X billion" — TAM is not demand
- "Once people see it, they'll understand" — if you have to explain the problem, it might not exist
- "Our competitor raised $X" — competitor funding is not demand validation

**Follow-up probes:**
- How much time/money are people currently spending on this problem?
- If you took away their current solution, what would they say?
- Can you name 3 specific people who would use this tomorrow?

---

## 2. Status Quo

**The question:** "What are people doing today without your solution, and why is that not good enough?"

**What good answers look like:**
- "They're using spreadsheets and it takes 4 hours/week" — clear pain, quantifiable
- "They're using [competitor] but it doesn't handle [specific case]" — gap identified
- "They're not doing it at all because it's too hard" — latent demand (risky but high upside)

**Red flags:**
- "There's nothing out there" — there's ALWAYS something, even if it's bad. Find it.
- "The existing solutions are bad" — bad how? For whom? What specifically fails?
- No status quo means no urgency — people won't switch from nothing to something easily

---

## 3. Desperate Specificity

**The question:** "Who would use a broken, ugly, half-finished version of this and be grateful?"

**What good answers look like:**
- "Oncall engineers at startups with <50 employees who get paged at 3am"
- "Solo founders doing their own bookkeeping before they can afford an accountant"
- A specific person with a name who said "yes, I'd use that today"

**Red flags:**
- "Developers" — too broad. Which developers? What stack? What company size?
- "Enterprise companies" — who at the enterprise? The CTO? A platform engineer? A PM?
- Can't name a specific person or company — the target is imaginary

**Why this matters:**
If nobody would use a broken v1, your v1 scope is wrong. Either narrow the audience or narrow the feature set until someone desperate emerges.

---

## 4. Narrowest Wedge

**The question:** "What is the absolute minimum version that delivers the core value — and nothing else?"

**How to find it:**
1. List everything the product could do
2. Cross out everything that isn't in the critical path of the one core value
3. What's left? That's your wedge.
4. Now cross out half of what's left. Can it still work? If yes, you haven't narrowed enough.

**Calibration:**
- If your wedge takes more than 2 weeks to build, it's too wide
- If your wedge has more than 3 features, it's too wide
- If your wedge serves more than 1 user persona, it's too wide

**The test:** Can you describe the wedge in one sentence without using the word "and"?

---

## 5. Observation & Surprise

**The question:** "What have you observed about this problem that surprised you — something most people in the space don't know?"

**What good answers look like:**
- "Everyone assumes X but actually Y" — contrarian insight backed by evidence
- "I noticed that [behavior] happens way more than people think"
- A specific data point, user behavior, or market dynamic that isn't obvious

**Red flags:**
- Restating conventional wisdom — if everyone knows it, it's not an insight
- "AI will change everything" — too generic to be actionable
- No personal observation — the best insights come from direct experience

**Why this matters:**
Your unfair advantage is what you know that others don't. If you don't have a surprising observation, you're competing on execution alone — which is possible but harder.

---

## 6. Future-Fit

**The question:** "Will this matter more or less in 3 years? What changes in the world would make this irrelevant?"

**What good answers look like:**
- "This gets MORE important as [trend] accelerates" — riding a tailwind
- "Even if [technology] changes, the underlying need remains" — durable value
- "The only thing that kills this is if [specific unlikely event] happens"

**Red flags:**
- "AI will handle this" — if AI will solve the problem natively, you're building a bridge to nowhere
- Building on a platform that's declining (unless you're extracting value on the way down)
- Dependent on a single API/partnership that could change terms

**The 3-year test:**
Imagine it's 3 years from now. What does the world look like? Is your product more or less relevant? If less, you're building for today, not tomorrow.
