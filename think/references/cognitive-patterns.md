# CEO Cognitive Patterns — Strategic Thinking Reference

18 thinking patterns drawn from world-class leaders. Use these to challenge plans, evaluate decisions, and find blind spots.

---

## Decision-Making Patterns

### 1. Inversion (Charlie Munger)
Instead of asking "how do I succeed?", ask **"what would guarantee failure?"** Then avoid those things.

**Apply when:** Evaluating a plan. List the top 3 ways this could fail catastrophically. If the plan doesn't address any of them, it's incomplete.

### 2. Two-Way vs One-Way Doors (Jeff Bezos)
**One-way doors** are irreversible — take time to decide. **Two-way doors** are reversible — decide fast and iterate.

**Apply when:** The team is agonizing over a decision. Ask: "Can we undo this?" If yes, stop debating and try it.

### 3. Disagree and Commit (Jeff Bezos)
You can disagree with a decision but still commit fully to executing it. Disagreement during discussion is healthy. Disagreement during execution is sabotage.

**Apply when:** There's no clear consensus but a decision must be made. Someone commits, everyone executes.

### 4. Regret Minimization (Jeff Bezos)
Project yourself to age 80. Will you regret NOT doing this? If yes, do it. Optimize for minimizing regret, not maximizing short-term outcome.

**Apply when:** The safe option and the ambitious option are both viable. Choose the one you won't regret.

## Product Patterns

### 5. Customer Obsession (Jeff Bezos)
Work **backward** from what the customer needs, not forward from what you can build. Start with the press release, then build the product.

**Apply when:** You're excited about a technology and looking for a problem to solve. Stop. Find the problem first.

### 6. Do Things That Don't Scale (Paul Graham)
In the early stage, manually do what you'll eventually automate. Recruit users one by one. Solve problems by hand. This gives you information that scales.

**Apply when:** Someone says "but that won't scale." Respond: "It doesn't need to scale yet. It needs to work."

### 7. 10x vs 10% (Andy Grove)
A 10% improvement doesn't change behavior — people won't switch from their current solution. A 10x improvement forces a switch because ignoring it is irrational.

**Apply when:** Evaluating whether your solution is compelling enough. If it's only marginally better, rethink the approach or narrow the audience until it IS 10x.

### 8. Make Something People Want (Paul Graham)
The most common startup killer is building something nobody wants. It's not about what's technically possible — it's about what people will actually use.

**Apply when:** Always. This is the meta-pattern above all others.

### 9. Taste (Steve Jobs)
Taste is not subjective preference — it's the ability to recognize quality and reject mediocrity. Having taste means knowing when something is "good enough" and when it isn't.

**Apply when:** Reviewing output quality. Would you personally use this? Would you show it to someone you respect?

## Execution Patterns

### 10. Only the Paranoid Survive (Andy Grove)
Strategic inflection points — moments when the fundamentals of your business change — are existential. The ones who survive are the ones who see them early.

**Apply when:** A major platform shift, competitor move, or market change appears. Don't dismiss it. Evaluate whether it changes the fundamentals.

### 11. Wartime vs Peacetime (Ben Horowitz)
**Peacetime:** stable market, clear advantage — focus on expanding, culture, process. **Wartime:** existential threat — focus on survival, speed, breaking rules.

**Apply when:** Deciding urgency. If you're in peacetime but acting like wartime, you'll burn out the team. If you're in wartime but acting like peacetime, you'll die.

### 12. The Hard Thing (Ben Horowitz)
There are no easy answers to hard problems. The CEO's job is to make the decision that no one else wants to make — with incomplete information and irreversible consequences.

**Apply when:** A decision feels impossible. It probably is hard. Make it anyway. Indecision is worse than a wrong decision.

### 13. No Rules Rules (Reed Hastings)
Remove process and rules when you have high-talent teams. Context, not control. Give people information and trust them to make good decisions.

**Apply when:** You're adding process to prevent a mistake. Consider instead: can you hire/develop people who don't need the process?

## Strategic Patterns

### 14. First Principles (Elon Musk/Physics thinking)
Boil things down to fundamental truths, then reason up from there. Most "knowledge" is convention — assumptions layered on assumptions. Peel them back.

**Apply when:** Everyone says "that's how it's done." Ask: "Why? What if that assumption is wrong?"

### 15. Fewer Things, Better (Dieter Rams)
The best products do fewer things with more quality. Complexity is the enemy. If you can remove something without losing value, remove it.

**Apply when:** A product or plan feels bloated. Apply the rule: remove features until it breaks. The last feature you removed before it broke? Put that back. Everything else stays out.

### 16. Design Thinking (Airbnb/Brian Chesky)
Go to the extremes. Design the 11-star experience, then work backward to what's feasible. Starting from "good enough" produces mediocrity.

**Apply when:** Designing user experience. Don't ask "what can we build?" Ask "what would blow their mind?" Then negotiate back to reality.

### 17. Compounding (Warren Buffett)
Small advantages, consistently applied, compound into massive advantages. Rushing for quick wins often destroys compounding.

**Apply when:** Evaluating speed vs quality trade-offs. Sometimes the slower approach compounds better.

### 18. Pre-mortem (Gary Klein)
Before starting, imagine the project has failed. Why did it fail? This is more effective than post-mortem analysis because it happens before you're emotionally invested.

**Apply when:** Starting any significant project. Spend 15 minutes on "imagine this failed — what went wrong?" The answers become your risk mitigation plan.
